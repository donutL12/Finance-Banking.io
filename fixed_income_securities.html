Fixed Income Securities

Stable, reliable investments with predictable income streams for your portfolio

Fixed income securities are investments that provide a consistent stream of income through regular interest payments and return of principal at maturity. These investments are ideal for conservative investors seeking stability and predictable returns in their portfolio.

Our Fixed Income Products

Government Bonds

Secure and stable investments backed by the government

  • Treasury bills (short-term)
  • Treasury notes (medium-term)
  • Treasury bonds (long-term)
  • Government-backed securities
  • Municipal bonds (tax advantages)

Government bonds offer the highest level of security with predictable returns, making them ideal for risk-averse investors seeking steady income.

Corporate Bonds

Higher yield investments backed by corporations

  • Investment-grade corporate bonds
  • High-yield corporate bonds
  • Convertible bonds
  • Commercial paper
  • Floating-rate notes

Corporate bonds typically offer higher yields than government securities, providing an opportunity for enhanced returns with varying levels of risk.

Fixed Income Funds

Diversified portfolios of fixed income securities

  • Government bond funds
  • Corporate bond funds
  • Money market funds
  • Short-term bond funds
  • Income-focused ETFs

Fixed income funds offer instant diversification across multiple bonds and issuers, providing a balanced approach to fixed income investing with professional management.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are fixed income securities?

Fixed income securities are investments that pay a fixed amount of interest, usually on a regular schedule, and return the principal investment at maturity. Common examples include government bonds, corporate bonds, and certificates of deposit. They're called "fixed income" because they provide a reliable, steady stream of income through interest payments.

Why invest in fixed income securities?

Fixed income securities offer several benefits, including regular income streams, capital preservation, portfolio diversification, and reduced overall portfolio volatility. They're particularly attractive for risk-averse investors, those nearing retirement, or anyone seeking predictable returns and stability in their investment portfolio.

How do interest rate changes affect bond prices?

Bond prices and interest rates have an inverse relationship. When interest rates rise, the prices of existing bonds typically fall because newer bonds are issued with higher interest rates, making the older, lower-rate bonds less attractive. Conversely, when interest rates fall, existing bonds with higher interest rates become more valuable, causing their prices to rise.

What's the difference between government and corporate bonds?

Government bonds are issued by the government and are generally considered the safest fixed income investments because they're backed by the full faith and credit of the issuing government. Corporate bonds are issued by companies and typically offer higher yields but come with higher risk, as their value depends on the financial health of the issuing corporation.

What is bond credit rating?

Bond credit ratings are assessments of the creditworthiness of a bond issuer, indicating the likelihood that the issuer will repay the bond's principal and interest. Independent rating agencies like Standard & Poor's, Moody's, and Fitch assign ratings ranging from AAA (highest quality) to D (in default). Higher-rated bonds generally offer lower yields but greater security, while lower-rated bonds typically offer higher yields to compensate for the increased risk.
